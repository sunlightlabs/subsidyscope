1 A subsidy is conceptually defined as the net benefit a recipient receives from government spending intended to support an economic activity. Subsidy estimates will be presented in varying formats based on availability. These formats include budgetary costs which may be net or gross of administrative costs, credit subsidy costs and tax expenditure revenue loss estimates. See Subsidyscope Methodology for more.
2 Spending on programs that contain a subsidy is the amount spent in a category less spending on programs that are unlikely to contain a subsidy (see below). For more on what programs Subsidyscope includes, see Subsidyscope Methodology.

Sources of error: Spending estimates rely on government data which have omissions and can be inaccurate. Overall we believe the estimates to be lower bounds due to missing data in federal data sets. In cases where we know the omissions are significant, such as subsidy estimates for direct expenditures, we will not supply an estimate but insert a question mark (“?”). Most spending is measured in obligations but some is only available in outlays . Thus they cannot strictly be added together. Subsidyscope does not consider programs that provide funding to States solely for the purpose of improving safety or enforcing federal laws as subsidy programs.
